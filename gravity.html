Gravity Explained

Gravity is a fundamental force that governs how objects with mass interact across space. It explains why objects fall toward the Earth and why planets orbit stars. Gravity influences the motion of moons, asteroids, and galaxies throughout the universe. On Earth, gravity affects everyday activities, from walking to the flow of water in rivers. Isaac Newton described gravity as a force acting at a distance between masses. Later, Albert Einstein showed that gravity results from the curvature of spacetime. Gravity is essential for the formation of stars and the stability of solar systems. It also causes tides due to the gravitational pull of the Moon and Sun. Modern science studies gravity using physics, astronomy, and cosmology. Understanding gravity helps advance space exploration and satellite technology.

Gravity Queries Answered

What is gravity?

Gravity is the fundamental force of attraction that exists between any two objects with mass. The more massive an object, the stronger its gravitational pull.

Who discovered gravity?

While the concept of objects falling was always observed, Sir Isaac Newton is credited with formulating the law of universal gravitation in the 17th century.

How does gravity affect planets?

Gravity is what keeps planets in orbit around stars and moons in orbit around planets, maintaining the structure of solar systems.

Is gravity a force or spacetime curvature?

According to Einstein's theory of general relativity, gravity is not a force but a curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy.

Can gravity be manipulated?

Currently, manipulating gravity is beyond our technological capabilities, though it remains a subject of theoretical physics and science fiction.
